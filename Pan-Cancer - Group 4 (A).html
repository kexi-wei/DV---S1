2. DESCRIPTION OF DATA SET

2.1. Where and why was the information collected?

The information collected used in this project was extracted from the supplementary table S1 of the research paper called ‘Pan-cancer analysis of whole genomes’ supervised by Peter J. Campbell, Gad Getz, Jan O. Korbel, Joshua M. Stuart and Lincoln D. Stein.

The experiment aims to better understand the mutations that drive tumors by sequencing whole genomes and including non-coding regions, structural variants, telomere regions… in opposition to the majority of tumor studies which only sequences the coding part of DNA. The information was collected in order to study specific genetic variations along the population which could lead to the development of cancer.

In order to do this, the scientists sequenced thousands of whole genomes and identified and classified each individual mutation. They later categorized them by tumor types amongst other variables.

The data used for the experiment came from big cancer databases such as the International Cancer Genome Consortium (ICGC) and The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA).

In this case, the data contains 2,658 whole-cancer genomes and their matching normal tissues across 38 tumour types from the Pan-Cancer Analysis of Whole Genomes (PCAWG) Consortium of the International Cancer Genome Consortium (ICGC) and The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA).

2.2. Which is the meaning of each variable?

From the 61 variables, we have chosen the 17 variables which are most important in order to explain our paper.

  • Tumour_specimen_aliquot_id: Identifier of the large sample of tumour specimen

  • DCC_specimen_type: Kind of tumor

  • Gender: Biological birth sex

  • Age: Years of the patient at the time

  • Organ_system: Origin organ of the tumour cells

  • Ancestry_afr: Proportion of African ancestry

  • Ancestry_asn: Proportion of Asian ancestry

  • Ancestry_amr: Proportion of American ancestry

  • Ancestry_san: Proportion of South Asian ancestry

  • Ancestry_eur: Proportion of European ancestry

  • Ancestry_primary: Dominant ancestry

  • Ancestry_primary_contribution: Percentage contribution of the dominant ancestry

  • Coding.SNVs: Number of SNVs in coding regions

  • Non.coding.SNVs: Number of SNVs in non-coding regions

  • Mitochondrial.mutations: Number of mutations in mitochondrial DNA

  • Tumour_grade: Degree of tumor aggressiveness

  • Percentage_cellularity: Percentage of tumour cells in the sample

  • All.SNVs: Total number of SNVs

2.3. Do the variables have unit? Which one?

Most of them do not have a unit because they are mostly categories (such as tumour specimen aliquot id, dcc specimen type, gender, organ system and primary ancestry), but there are a few of them that they do:

  • Age: Years

  • Ancestry_afr: Proportion of ancestry between 0-1

  • Ancestry_asn: Proportion of ancestry between 0-1

  • Ancestry_amr: Proportion of ancestry between 0-1

  • Ancestry_san: Proportion of ancestry between 0-1

  • Ancestry_eur: Proportion of ancestry between 0-1

  • Ancestry_primary_contribution: Proportion of ancestry between 0-1

  • Coding.SNVs: Number of mutations

  • Non.coding.SNVs: Number of mutations

  • Mitochondrial.mutations: Number of mutations

  • Percentage_cellularity: Percentage over 100

  • All.SNVs: Number of SNVs

2.4. Does the data set have a long format?

For a wide-format data, multiple measurements of a single observation (a single patient) are stored in a row (e.g. age, ancestry, coding.SNVs… of the same patient are in one row but different columns).

Whereas for long-format data frames, every single information of an observation/patient is presented in a different row (e.g. age, ancestry, coding.SNVs… of the same patient are in different rows). So, taking a look to the data frame we got, it can clearly be seen that the data set does not have a long format, instead, a wide format is presented.

After showing the variation of the gender variable between organ systems, now we look for the difference in the total number of SNVs among them. To do so, we have used the geom_point() function, so that we can see all the SNVs obtained in each patient for each organ.

So, with the plot, we are showing two things: the variability of SNVs within each organ system, but also between them, as there is a much difference between the SNVs observed in, for example, Skin or Large Intestine and the SNVs in Blood, Bone, Marrow & Hematopoietic Sys or Thyroid Gland. The two points with the more SNVs are located in Rectum and Large Intestine, while the lowest is in Blood, Bone, Marrow & Hematopoietic Sys.

Another important point, is that we can see that some organ-specific tumors have a very limited range of SNVs for the tumor to appear and therefore a consistent mutation level (e.g. Gallbladder & Extrahepatic Bile Ducts). Other organs such as Stomach and Brain, however, have a wide range of SNVs observed in patients, making it harder to approximate the number of SNVs that makes a tumor appear.

Also, we can extrapolate that the organs with more points seen towards the left (less SNVs) represent tumors that are easier to get as they need less SNVs to appear. Therefore, based on this hypothesis, a Large Intestine tumor is harder to get while a Thyroid Gland tumor is relatively easier to get.

Hypothesis 2. Following the previous hypothesis, males have higher tendency to develop cancer, which is directly caused by a more number of mutations in their DNA sequence.

Conclusions

To check for the number of mutations in each gender, we have designed a boxplot showing the total SNVs in a log10 scale (to reshape the graph such that is more understandable) between the two genders, so a statistical difference is distinguishable. In addition, we have added the significance level of the variables.

As we can observe, the females’ box plot is slightly more shifted to the left, meaning that statistically, they have less SNV than males. Furthermore, taking a look to the p-value (0.018) shown in the plot, it can be stated that this difference is significant, such that males are indeed also more probable to experiment more mutations in the DNA.

Biologically, this could be extremely linked to the previous hypothesis, as cancerigenous tissues are caused precisely by mutations on its cells, which leads to cancer. Therefore, as males are generally statistically more probable to have a mutation, it is reasonable to expect that they also have prevalence to develop most types cancer.
